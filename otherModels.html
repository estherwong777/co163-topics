Other Models (Lambertian, Phong..)

Phong Reflection Model

This is an early model, the paper for which was published in 1975.

The Phong reflection model is an empirical model based on physical observations: in particular, Phong's observations regarding specular reflection and specular highlight. Specular highlights occur on an object where there is a bright spot on the material at a certain viewing angle. For metals, this is especially pronounced, due to its smooth and shiny surface, creating more distinct highlights, and most of the light reflection on metals is composed of specular reflection.

From his observations, Phong determined that the specular highlight was smaller and the edges more distinct for shiny surfaces than for more dull surfaces, where the highlight was larger and had a less distinct edge.

In Phong's model, light reflection has three components: specular reflection, diffuse reflection, and ambient reflection, which are all summed to form the final BRDF.

Diffuse reflection is affected by the texture of the suface of the object, and the effect of it is greater in objects which are dull with rough surfaces, as the microfacets in the surface will reflect light in many directions. Ambient light is equal in all directions, and so all surfaces are illuminated equally by ambient light. For each of these components, a constant coefficient is defined, the value of which is dependent on the incident light and the material:

  • ks - specular reflection coefficient
  • kd - diffuse reflection coefficient
  • ka - ambient reflection coefficient

In an RGB model, the equation can be used to model the intensity of each colour separately, altering the ks, kd and ka constants depending on the colour.

Advantages of the Phong model

The main advantage of the Phong model is that it is fairly simple to compute, and so provides an efficient way of rendering light reflectance on surfaces, whilst also being relatively simple to implement. This has made it very popular to use. Other models have built on Phong's model, such as the Blinn-Phong model, which makes the assumption that the viewer and light source are at infinity, speeding up the computation time.

Limitations of the Phong model

One problem with using the Phong model to render scratched metals is that it assumes specular highlights are of the same colour as the light source, which does not hold true for metals in particular, although it is true for dielectrics. Other more advanced models, such as the Cook-Torrance model do not suffer from this problem, as they take the colour contribution from the surface into account, making the Phong model weaker at rendering metals in comparison.

Another issue with the Phong model is that it does not satisfy one of the main BRDF properties, as it is not energy conserving, creating more light than it receives. This means the Phong model is physically incorrect, as it is based on physical observation rather than physics, using Phong's informal observations of light reflectance. For example, the constants and ks which are used to model specular reflectance have no physical meaning, instead being adjusted by the user to achieve the highlight intensity they want.

The Phong model also does not satisfy the reciprocal property of a BRDF, as the surface is modelled to reflect light in one directly different than in the other, when the angle of incidence and reflected angles are swapped. This is not physically possible, and so means the Phong model is physically incorrect.

Additional/Phong shading

In Phong's paper, he also described a method for rendering polygons using interpolation, known as Phong shading, which produces smoother shading of surfaces, reducing the Mach Band effect, where the contrast of colour between polygon edges is exaggerated. This technique is still used today in rendering.

Lambertian Model

What is a Lambertian surface?

The Lambertian model emulates the reflection of a Lambertian surface. Surfaces that are Lambertian have the same radiance from any direction of view, meaning that it’s radiance is isotropic and that the surface reflects light equally in all directions. Surfaces which are Lambertian can be also be called ideal diffusion surfaces or ideal matte surfaces. [1]


Lambert's Cosine Law

All Lambertian surfaces obey this law. This law states that the radiant intensity observed from an ideally diffusely reflecting surface is directly proportional to the cosine of angle Θ, which is between the surface normal and the observer’s line of sight.

Evaluation of the Lambertian Model

One of the assumptions of this model is that the surface is a perfect diffuse reflector. In real life, these materials don't exist. Some of the light, in reality, may be absorbed by the surface. Alternatively, some light may be reflected in a reduced specular reflection.

The Lambertian Model is good for modelling matte surfaces such as clay, paper etc. Metals tend to have more specular reflections due to it's reflective nature, hence, the Lambertian model may not be the right model to use to render metals.
